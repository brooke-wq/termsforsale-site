How a subject-to deal works — deed transfers, mortgage stays

Subject-to real estate — often called "sub-to" — is one of the most powerful tools in a creative investor's toolkit. It lets you acquire a property without getting a new mortgage, by taking over the seller's existing loan. The rate, balance, and terms stay in place. You get the deed. The seller moves on.

In today's market, where rates are sitting well above 6%, the ability to step into a 2.5% or 3% loan from 2020 or 2021 is a massive advantage. You can't create that rate anywhere else. You can only inherit it through a sub-to transaction.

How Subject-To Works — The Basics

In a traditional home purchase, you apply for a new mortgage, qualify based on your income and credit, and pay today's interest rate. In a subject-to deal, none of that happens.

Instead, here's what happens:

  1. The seller deeds the property to you (or your entity)
  2. The existing mortgage stays in place in the seller's name
  3. You take over making the monthly payments on that mortgage
  4. You own and control the property — you can rent it, refi it, or sell it
Key Point

The loan stays in the seller's name. The deed transfers to you. This is what makes sub-to different from every other acquisition strategy — and why it requires trust, communication, and a solid agreement between both parties.

Why Would a Seller Agree to This?

This is the first question most people ask. The answer is simpler than you'd think: sellers in certain situations care more about speed and relief than maximizing their sale price.

  • Behind on payments — a sub-to gets them out before foreclosure hits their credit
  • Divorce or estate — they need to split assets fast without a drawn-out MLS process
  • Job relocation — they've already moved and are carrying two mortgages
  • Inherited property — heirs don't want to deal with maintenance or a long sale
  • Negative equity — the property is worth less than the loan, so a traditional sale nets them nothing

In each case, the seller gets what they actually need: a clean exit, fast, with no commissions and no open houses.

What Is the Due-On-Sale Clause?

Every mortgage has a due-on-sale clause — a provision that technically allows the lender to call the loan due if ownership of the property transfers. This is the most-discussed risk in sub-to transactions.

In practice, lenders rarely invoke this clause as long as payments are being made consistently. Banks are in the business of receiving payments, not managing properties. A loan performing perfectly is the last thing they want to disrupt.

That said, investors should understand this risk exists, work with a creative finance attorney, and have a plan for what happens in the unlikely event a lender does call the loan.

Subject-To vs. Other Creative Finance Strategies

  • Sub-To vs. Assumption — A loan assumption is a formal process where the lender approves the transfer and the new buyer qualifies. Sub-to is informal — the transfer happens without lender involvement.
  • Sub-To vs. Seller Finance — In seller finance, the seller owns the property free and clear and acts as the lender. In sub-to, there's an existing bank mortgage that stays in place.
  • Sub-To vs. Lease Option — A lease option gives you the right to purchase later. Sub-to gives you the deed now.

Who Should Be Buying Sub-To Deals

  • Buy and hold investors who want cash flow with low entry
  • Investors targeting markets where today's rates kill cash flow
  • Anyone who wants to lock in a below-market rate that no lender will match
  • Experienced investors comfortable with creative finance structures
  • Investors with reserves and a 5+ year hold horizon
